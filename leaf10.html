Phishing: The Evolving Threat

November 2025

Right, let's talk about phishing. I know, I know - you've probably heard this a thousand times before. "Don't click suspicious links," "Check the sender's email address," blah blah blah. But here's the thing: if you think phishing is still just those dodgy emails from Nigerian princes, you're in for a rude awakening. As someone who deals with these attacks daily, I can tell you that phishing has evolved dramatically, and the scammers are getting scary good at what they do.

When I started in cybersecurity a few years ago, spotting a phishing email was almost laughably easy. Terrible spelling, weird formatting, obviously fake sender addresses - they practically screamed "SCAM!" at you. But today? I've seen phishing emails that would fool even the most cautious person. We're talking perfect grammar, legitimate-looking branding, and sender addresses that are nearly identical to the real thing.

The New Face of Phishing


Let me paint you a picture of what modern phishing looks like. Last month, we dealt with an attack that targeted one of our clients through a fake Microsoft Teams notification. The email looked absolutely spot-on - proper logo, correct formatting, even the right shade of purple. It claimed someone had shared an urgent document and prompted the user to "verify their identity" to view it. The link didn't even look suspicious at first glance. That's the level of sophistication we're dealing with now.

But here's where it gets really scary: Business Email Compromise, or BEC for short. This is hands-down the biggest threat we're seeing right now, and it's particularly nasty because the emails ARE genuine. Let me explain. Imagine you regularly receive invoices from a supplier you've worked with for years. One day, you get an email from them - from their actual email address, with their usual tone and formatting - asking you to update their payment details for future invoices. You'd probably do it without question, right?

That's exactly what happened to one company we worked with. The supplier's email account had been compromised, and the attackers were sitting inside their inbox, monitoring conversations and waiting for the perfect moment to strike. The email came from a legitimate, trusted source because the scammers had hijacked it. There was no malware, no suspicious links - just a simple request that seemed perfectly normal. The company lost £45,000 before anyone realised something was wrong.



Understanding How Phishing Actually Works


Here's something crucial that most people don't realise: the phishing email itself usually isn't the thing that causes damage. It's not carrying some Hollywood-style virus that's going to fry your computer the moment you open it. Instead, phishing emails are gateways – they're designed to trick you into voluntarily handing over your login credentials, personal information, or payment details.

Think of it like this: a burglar doesn't need to break your window if they can convince you to hand them the keys. That's what phishing does. You click a link that takes you to a fake login page that looks identical to the real thing. You type in your email and password because you think you're logging into your bank, or Microsoft 365, or Amazon. And just like that, the attackers have what they need to access your actual accounts. No malware required.

Sometimes they'll ask you to download an "important document" or "security update" - that's when malicious software might come into play. But more often, they're after your credentials because those are the keys to everything else.



A Different Way to Think About Email


Here's an analogy that really helped one of my non-technical friends understand why we need to be so cautious: imagine if every email you received was actually a stranger walking up to you on the street and reading you the exact same message.

Picture it: someone approaches you in town and says, "Hello, I'm from your bank. We've noticed suspicious activity on your account and need you to verify your details immediately by following me to this website." They're saying all the right words, maybe even wearing a lanyard, but they look nothing like anyone from your actual bank. You'd never follow them, would you? You'd probably tell them to do one and call your bank yourself.

That's exactly how you should treat emails, even ones that appear to come from legitimate companies or people you know. The email might say all the right things, have the correct logo, and sound professional - but you can't actually see who's on the other end. Just like that stranger on the street, they could be anyone. Before you hand over any information, change any payment details, or click any links, stop and verify through a channel you control. It's the digital equivalent of walking away from that stranger and calling your bank directly.



Why This Matters to You


Here's what keeps me up at night: it's not just about losing access to your email or social media account anymore. Modern phishing attacks can lead to identity theft, financial loss, ransomware infections, and even compromise your entire digital life. I've seen people lose thousands of pounds, have their photos held for ransom, and spend months trying to recover their identity after falling for a sophisticated phishing attack.

And if you think "it won't happen to me," think again. The attackers aren't just targeting tech-illiterate people. They're going after everyone - young, old, tech-savvy or not. I've seen IT professionals get caught out by particularly clever attacks. Nobody is immune.

Phishing has also spread beyond just email. We're seeing text messages (smishing), voice calls (vishing), social media DMs, and even QR codes used as attack vectors. Scammers impersonate delivery companies via text, tax authorities through phone calls, and friends on WhatsApp asking for emergency money transfers. They're adapting to wherever we're paying attention.



Staying Ahead of the Scammers


**Slow down. ** Seriously, this is the most important one. Scammers rely on you acting quickly without thinking. If something feels urgent, that's exactly when you should pause and double-check.

**Verify independently.** If you get an email, text, or call asking for sensitive information or payment details - even from a known contact - verify it through a different channel. Call them using a number you already have saved, or send them a fresh email or message. Never use contact details provided in a suspicious message.

**Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) everywhere.** This is your safety net. Even if a scammer gets your password through phishing, MFA makes it much harder for them to actually access your account. Use an authenticator app rather than SMS codes when possible - they're more secure.

**Be suspicious of credential requests.** Legitimate companies very rarely ask you to log in via a link in an email. If you need to access your account, type the website address directly into your browser or use your saved bookmark.

**Trust your gut.** If something feels off, it probably is. That email from your bank asking you to verify your details? Dodgy. That text claiming you've won a competition you never entered? Scam. Your instincts are often right.

**Keep everything updated. ** Your phone, computer, apps - all of it. Updates often include security patches that protect against known phishing and malware techniques.


Look, I'm not trying to make you paranoid about every email you receive. But the reality is that phishing has evolved from obvious scams to sophisticated, targeted attacks that can fool almost anyone. The good news? By understanding how these attacks work and taking a few simple precautions, you can dramatically reduce your risk.

Stay skeptical, stay curious, and stay safe out there. The scammers are getting smarter, but so can we.
